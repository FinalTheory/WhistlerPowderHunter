------

Task: THERMAL_PHASE_TASK

Condition:
Trigger this task when precipitation is present or likely in the short term (0-72h), and thermal structure may materially change precipitation phase or snow quality at Whistler ski-relevant elevations.

Inputs:
- Regional precip-phase graphics with 1000-500 mb thickness preferred.
- Freezing level / snowline related fields if available.
- Supporting large-scale fields only when needed to interpret trend direction.
- Multiple models may be provided, with uneven UTC coverage and lead-time length.

Goal:
Assess whether ongoing or upcoming precipitation will fall as dry snow, dense/wet snow, mixed phase, or rain at Whistler ski elevations, with primary focus on upper mountain and alpine (~1600-2200 m).
Provide a clear, decision-oriented phase-risk interpretation tied to timing windows.

Analysis focus:
1. Thermal trend through event window
   - Is the thermal profile cooling, warming, or oscillating during precipitation?
   - Identify key transition windows rather than single-point values.
   - Distinguish brief warm spikes from sustained warming.

2. Phase risk at ski-relevant elevations
   - Evaluate likely phase at upper mountain/alpine first, then mid-mountain only if decision-relevant.
   - Classify thermal impact as:
     - FAVORABLE_COLD: phase supports dry or reasonably light snow
     - MARGINAL_WARM: denser or wetter snow likely, but still mostly snow at key elevations
     - PHASE_RISK: mixed/rain risk or clear quality degradation in key ski zones
   - Explicitly note if rain risk is mostly low-elevation-limited versus extending into upper terrain.
   - For powder-oriented decisions, dense wet storm snow is a quality downgrade, not a positive snowfall signal.

3. Thickness-based interpretation
   - Use 1000-500 mb thickness contours as trend guidance, not deterministic truth.
   - Apply these empirical anchors:
     - 552 dam: ~2500 m freezing level
     - 546 dam: ~2000 m freezing level
     - 540 dam: ~1500 m freezing level
     - 534 dam: ~1000 m freezing level
     - 528 dam: ~500 m freezing level
     - 522 dam: ~sea level freezing level
   - Prioritize contour movement direction and persistence over exact contour placement at one timestamp.

4. Model convergence and structural disagreement
   - Compare models on thermal trend direction, transition timing, and phase envelope.
   - Distinguish minor timing noise from structural disagreement (e.g., one model warms aloft while others cool).
   - Assign qualitative convergence level (HIGH / MED / LOW) with brief justification.

5. Decision relevance
   - State whether phase trend is improving, degrading, or neutral for near-term ski quality.
   - Highlight what specific change would flip the interpretation (e.g., sustained 546 dam push inland, delayed cooling onset).
   - If a warm atmospheric-river / pineapple-express-type setup is producing wet snow, mixed rain/snow, or near/above freezing temperatures at key ski elevations, say explicitly that this argues against a worthwhile powder day even if snowfall amounts remain moderate or heavy.
   - Keep this task focused on thermal interpretation and quality risk; do not emit final GO / GO_WITH_RISK / NO_GO labels unless DECISION_TASK is also being executed elsewhere.

Decision logic:
- If thermal structure is stable and clearly cold during precip, finalize without extra image requests.
- If the already-provided evidence is sufficient to show a warm atmospheric-river / pineapple-express-type setup is already compromising worthwhile powder quality, finalize without requesting extra bridge frames just to pin down the exact transition hour.
- If phase hinges on a narrow timing boundary or model disagreement, request the minimum additional images needed around the transition window.

Notes:
- Keep analysis trend-based and conservative; avoid overconfidence in deterministic freezing-level numbers.
- Do not overemphasize valley/mid-mountain warmth when alpine remains clearly in snow phase.
- Also do not understate quality degradation when upper-mountain and key ski-elevation sensors are near or above freezing during active precipitation; in that case, emphasize wet-snow / mixed-phase risk clearly.
- Mention uncertainty explicitly whenever phase classification depends on unresolved short-term timing.
